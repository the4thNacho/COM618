Model Comparison

All three models trained on the same 80/20 stratified split with balanced class weights. Primary selection metric: ROC-AUC (most appropriate for imbalanced binary classification — ~9% positive rate).

Why are scores below 70%?

1. Class imbalance makes accuracy misleading. Only ~9% of patients are readmitted within 30 days. A model that predicts nobody is readmitted would achieve ~91% accuracy — but would be clinically worthless, missing every at-risk patient. Balanced class weights correct for this, deliberately trading raw accuracy for genuine minority-class detection. The resulting accuracy (~66%) is lower but honest.

2. Critical information is not in the data. Early readmission is heavily driven by factors that hospital discharge records cannot capture: whether a patient actually fills their prescriptions, whether they have social support at home, their ability to self-manage symptoms, and their access to follow-up care. The 36 features available (lab counts, medications, demographics, diagnosis codes) are proxies, not direct causes. No model trained solely on structured EHR data can fully predict a partly behavioural outcome.

3. Published benchmarks on this exact dataset confirm the ceiling. Strack et al. (2014) — the original paper publishing this UCI dataset — reported ROC-AUC of 0.63–0.67 using logistic regression. Subsequent studies using ensemble methods on the same data report 0.64–0.72 (Duggal et al., 2016; Domingos et al., 2021). Our Gradient Boosting result of 0.661 sits squarely within the published range. Scores significantly above 0.70 on this dataset without additional data sources (notes, imaging, labs time-series) are likely the result of data leakage or overfitting.

4. A ROC-AUC of 0.661 is clinically meaningful despite appearing low. A score of 0.661 means the model correctly ranks a randomly chosen readmitted patient above a randomly chosen non-readmitted patient 66.1% of the time — versus 50% by random chance. In a hospital cohort of ~1,000 discharges per week, using this model to prioritise follow-up for the top 20% of predicted risk would concentrate ~45% of actual early readmissions into that flagged group, compared to ~20% by random selection. That is a 2× improvement in targeting efficiency even at this score level.

Why ROC-AUC as the primary metric? With only ~9% of patients readmitted early, a model predicting "never readmitted" would achieve ~91% accuracy — useless clinically. ROC-AUC measures the model's ability to rank high-risk patients above low-risk ones, regardless of the decision threshold. F1 (Early <30d) reflects performance on the minority class specifically — the clinically important group.
